# The R&D Ratchet

## Editorial plan for a blog series that can become a book

**Recommended series title:** *The R&D Ratchet*

**Recommended book title:** *The R&D Ratchet: How America Lost the Institutions That Could Think Long—and the Narrow Window to Build What Comes Next*

**Alternative title:** *The Institutions That Think Long: An Inside Account from Bell Labs to the AI-Native Laboratory*

**One-sentence promise:** A firsthand and evidence-driven account of how the United States replaced long-horizon research institutions with narrower incentive systems that remain productive in isolation but may be collectively incapable of maintaining the scientific and engineering capabilities on which future innovation depends.

## The non-trivial thesis

The story is not that the United States stopped spending money on R&D. That claim would be false and would make the series easy to dismiss. U.S. R&D expenditure was an estimated $993.4 billion in 2024, but experimental development accounted for 67.3 percent while basic research accounted for 14.6 percent. The important question is what the aggregate category “R&D” conceals.

The stronger thesis is:

> The United States did not simply defund research. It replaced one institutional equilibrium with several narrower incentive equilibria: quarterly corporate returns, finite government programs, billable contract research, academic publication metrics, patent accounting, and venture-scale exits. Each rewards valuable activity. None is reliably rewarded for maintaining the shared, stateful infrastructure of discovery: protected time, technical apprenticeship, multidisciplinary teams, specialized facilities, negative results, institutional memory, and the engineering capacity to carry an idea across multiple transitions.

The failure is therefore not mainly a shortage of ideas, talent, or even nominal R&D dollars. It is a coordination and incentives failure. The benefits of durable research infrastructure spill across firms, sectors, and countries, while its costs sit on the balance sheet of one organization. Every actor can behave rationally while the overall system consumes inherited capability faster than it replenishes it.

This also makes the decline path-dependent. A laboratory is not a budget plus a building. It contains tacit knowledge, trusted relationships, toolchains, experimental judgment, mentorship chains, and an accumulated ability to select important problems. Once those are dispersed, a later appropriation cannot buy the prior state back. Funding can restart projects; it cannot instantly restart an institution.

The book should distinguish two conclusions:

1. **There is probably no way back to the old settlement.** The monopoly rents, vertically integrated industrial systems, employment norms, and political bargains that supported the classical corporate laboratory no longer exist in the same form.
2. **There may be a narrow way forward.** One institutional generation may remain in which people who experienced the older laboratories can transfer their tacit knowledge into a successor model built around public–private capital, durable missions, shared infrastructure, and AI-native scientific work.

The global stakes matter. The old U.S. laboratories generated spillovers used throughout the world. If no successor institution can support work with broad benefits that no single company can capture, the loss is global even if the institutional failure begins in the United States.

## Why Karim is positioned to write it

The value is not proximity to famous names. It is movement through different incentive systems while doing related technical work:

- **2001 — Bell Labs/Lucent Technologies:** a summer internship during the telecom collapse, providing an early view of technical excellence inside a deteriorating corporate settlement.
- **2007 — AT&T Research Labs:** a research internship during graduate school, allowing comparison between two descendants of the Bell system rather than treating “Bell Labs” as one continuous abstraction.
- **2010–2016 — HRL Laboratories:** owner-sponsored and government-funded mission R&D; the local record documents more than $3.5 million in led work, two inventor awards, publications, and a substantial patent portfolio.
- **2017–2022 — SRI International:** independent nonprofit contract research, internal seed funding, and leadership of major DARPA programs spanning secure computation, formal verification, anonymous communication, and FHE acceleration.
- **2022–present — Confidencial.io:** direct experience of the transition from research and patents to a company, product, customers, procurement, and survival.
- **2023 onward — PARC at SRI:** an important institutional case observed as an SRI alumnus, but not a firsthand account of the acquisition or the combined organization.
- **2026 — IBM’s announced agreement to acquire HRL:** a current occasion to ask what consolidation preserves, what it changes, and why major research capabilities increasingly need a small number of institutional hosts.

This is approximately twenty-five years of firsthand participation, inside a thirty-year institutional arc from the 1996 Lucent spinout to the 2026 IBM–HRL announcement. Use that formulation instead of claiming thirty full years of personal employment.

The local website supplies an unusually rich evidence base: 79 cataloged research works, 31 granted patents, 12 funded R&D projects, open-source artifacts, awards, talks, and an existing “R&D Labyrinth” intellectual-lineage map. These should be used as evidence of how work changed under different systems, not as a list of accomplishments.

## Definitions that prevent a nostalgic or simplistic book

### R&D infrastructure

R&D infrastructure is the durable system that allows a society to repeatedly turn uncertainty into knowledge and knowledge into working capability. It includes:

- a mandate and a legitimate source of patient capital;
- researchers, engineers, technicians, program leaders, and apprentices;
- facilities, compute, fabrication, data, instruments, and toolchains;
- protected time and permission to pursue uncertain questions;
- organizational memory, including failures and abandoned paths;
- trusted links to customers, product groups, government missions, and users;
- mechanisms for transition, procurement, licensing, standards, and manufacturing;
- governance that can tolerate technical risk without tolerating unaccountability.

### Five different meanings of “success”

Never call a laboratory, program, or project simply a success or failure. Evaluate at least five layers:

1. **Scientific success:** Did it produce true, important, or reusable knowledge?
2. **Technical success:** Did it produce a working system or new capability?
3. **Transition success:** Did another organization adopt, manufacture, deploy, or maintain it?
4. **Institutional success:** Did the work leave behind stronger people, tools, memory, and problem-solving capacity?
5. **Economic or public-value success:** Did the sponsor, firm, country, or wider world obtain value commensurate with the investment?

A famous invention can be a scientific success and an institutional or capture failure. A terminated program can fail to deploy while still creating people and methods that succeed elsewhere. This separation should be one of the book’s signature analytical contributions.

## The recurring incentive-structure lens

Each article should analyze the same questions so the cases accumulate into an argument:

1. Who supplied the money, and what return could that actor capture?
2. What was the real time horizon—not the advertised one?
3. Who selected problems, and how close were they to operational users?
4. What behavior earned promotion, status, renewal, or survival?
5. Who paid for shared tools, facilities, and technical staff between projects?
6. What happened to people and knowledge when a project, program, or business cycle ended?
7. Who owned the IP, and who possessed the complementary assets needed to use it?
8. Was transition a funded phase or an unfunded hope?
9. What public or global value escaped the sponsor’s accounting?
10. Which capability survived five or ten years later?

The comparison should remain qualitative until sufficiently comparable data exist. A false numerical score would create precision without validity.

## The thirteen-article series

### 1. The Nearly Trillion-Dollar R&D System That Cannot Think Long

**Function:** Series manifesto and empirical paradox.

Open with IBM’s announced agreement to acquire HRL and the earlier transfer of PARC to SRI. Then complicate the decline narrative: America is spending almost a trillion dollars a year on activities classified as R&D. The problem is the composition, concentration, horizons, and institutional form of that activity.

**Central claim:** R&D expenditure is a flow. Research capability is accumulated state. National statistics measure the former much better than the latter.

**Personal anchor:** Seeing institutions from your own career reassembled under new owners or nonprofit hosts.

**Question left open:** Are these combinations renewal, rescue, concentration, or the final consolidation of capabilities that the broader system can no longer sustain?

### 2. I Arrived at Bell Labs as Lucent Was Coming Apart

**Function:** Human opening and the first incentive regime.

Reconstruct the 2001 internship as a scene: what technical quality felt like from inside, what organizational stress looked like, and what a young researcher could perceive without pretending to have seen the entire company.

**Central claim:** Scientific excellence and corporate fragility can coexist. A laboratory’s culture cannot indefinitely outrun the economics of its parent.

**Incentive mechanism:** The vertically integrated Bell system had linked research, manufacturing, network operations, and long-lived technical careers. Lucent inherited much of the research culture but faced a radically different capital market and telecom-equipment environment.

**Discipline:** Separate memory, public corporate history, and later inference. Do not retrofit every visible event into an inevitable collapse narrative.

### 3. One Heritage, Two Incentive Systems: Lucent and AT&T

**Function:** A natural comparison made possible by the 2001 and 2007 internships.

AT&T’s 1996 restructuring sent most of Bell Labs and equipment manufacturing to Lucent while a smaller research organization remained with the services business. Compare what changed when related researchers served different parent businesses.

**Central claim:** “Research culture” is not an independent variable. The parent’s customers, margins, asset base, regulatory history, product cycle, and ability to absorb inventions determine which research practices survive.

**Personal evidence:** The Lucent internship and later traffic-filtering work at AT&T Research with Bala Krishnamurthy and collaborators. Use the work to show problem selection and transition context, not to claim that two internships reveal every feature of either institution.

### 4. HRL and the Bargain of Mission-Driven Research

**Function:** Examine the sponsor-owned, government-customer laboratory.

Use the shift from Hughes to an HRL owned by Boeing and General Motors, followed by the announced IBM transaction, to study a laboratory supported by strategic owners and public missions rather than a mass-market product pipeline.

**Central claim:** A durable mission customer can protect technically ambitious work from consumer-market clocks, but sponsor priorities, contract boundaries, classification, and transition channels define the feasible research space.

**Personal evidence:** Secure search, proactive computation, resilient cloud systems, automotive and cyber-physical security, biometrics, Boeing-funded network analysis, inventor awards, papers, and patents from 2010–2016.

**Counterquestion:** Did mission-driven funding preserve a form of industrial research, or did it convert the laboratory into a portfolio of projects? The answer may differ by technical area and period.

### 5. SRI and the Project-Funded Institution

**Function:** Analyze independent nonprofit contract research.

SRI lacks a captive product division but can maintain deep intellectual communities across government and commercial programs. Its strengths and vulnerabilities are different from both Bell Labs and HRL.

**Central claim:** A nonprofit research institute can provide continuity across sponsors, but when most labor must remain chargeable to finite projects, proposal incentives and funding cliffs can quietly become the institution’s operating system.

**Personal evidence:** Internal seedlings; DARPA RACE, SIEVE, and DPRIVE; Air Force quantum work; formal methods; cryptography; and collaborations across universities, companies, and SRI groups.

**Important distinction:** PARC joined SRI in 2023, after your SRI tenure. Discuss that event through documents and interviews, explicitly as an alumnus and observer rather than an inside witness.

### 6. DARPA Can Create a Temporary Laboratory. What Happens When the Program Ends?

**Function:** Separate the program incentive from the performer institution.

DARPA program managers can assemble extraordinary cross-organizational teams, give them a hard technical objective, and force progress through milestones. That is a powerful invention. It is not the same thing as an enduring laboratory.

**Central claim:** The modern U.S. system is often excellent at creating temporary networks of capability and weak at retaining the shared state those networks accumulate after a program ends.

**Evidence:** Compare your experience across PRISM/RACE, EMPHASIZE/SIEVE, FARSIDE/DPRIVE, and earlier DHS/IARPA work. Trace what happened to people, code, test infrastructure, and transition prospects after each clock expired.

**Fairness:** The finite tenure of DARPA program managers and aggressive milestones are sources of urgency and strength, not defects by themselves. The failure occurs when no complementary institution is rewarded to catch and preserve what a successful program creates.

### 7. The University Is Not the Missing Laboratory

**Function:** Give academia its own incentive analysis rather than treating it as a footnote to corporate-laboratory decline.

**Central claim:** Universities are indispensable to basic research, open knowledge, and scientific training, but project grants, publication markets, degree clocks, and facilities-and-administrative reimbursement do not fund the complete institutional stack required for durable R&D.

**Evidence:** Use NCSES higher-education R&D composition and concentration; empirical comparisons of project and investigator funding; work on novelty, delayed recognition, and incentive selection; GAO analyses of grant burden and indirect costs; the 2025 NIH indirect-cost notice; and the January 2026 First Circuit opinion affirming the permanent injunction against that notice.

**Overhead discipline:** Reject both “all overhead is waste” and “negotiated rates prove efficient spending.” Explain modified total direct costs, the difference between facilities and administration, legitimate shared capability, administrative accretion, institutional cross-subsidy, and the feedback loop through which grant volume becomes necessary to sustain the grant system.

**Institutional implication:** Fund bounded projects, durable capability, and public accountability as distinct purchases. Do not ask graduate students, technology-transfer offices, or indirect-cost recovery to impersonate a permanent systems-and-transition organization.

### 8. PARC and the Appropriability Trap

**Function:** Replace the cliché that Xerox “failed to see the future” with a sharper economic question.

**Central claim:** Generating an invention, recognizing its importance, possessing complementary production and distribution assets, and capturing its value are four different capabilities. The research lab may control only the first.

Use the 2023 donation of PARC to SRI—including the disclosed allocation of patent rights—as a late institutional chapter, not merely another retelling of the graphical-user-interface story.

**Broader mechanism:** Knowledge spillovers make foundational research socially valuable and privately underfunded. This is precisely where government has a legitimate role, provided public funding does not merely socialize risk while privatizing all durable capability.

### 9. Papers, Patents, Prototypes—and Vanishing Capability

**Function:** Attack the output-accounting problem using your own record rather than someone else’s.

Your site lists 79 research works, 31 granted patents, 12 funded programs, open-source systems, awards, and a successful spinout. Ask which of these indicators measure knowledge, adoption, institutional learning, or public benefit—and which merely remain countable after the underlying team has dispersed.

**Central claim:** Output metrics are lossy projections of a stateful system. They can rise while the capacity to produce the next generation of work declines.

**Use the 2024 Test of Time Award carefully:** It is a rare retrospective signal of enduring influence, but even long-term citation or recognition does not by itself establish deployment or institutional continuity.

### 10. The Startup Is Not a Research Laboratory

**Function:** Examine venture and market incentives from inside technology transition.

**Central claim:** A startup can be an effective vehicle for carrying a specific invention through productization, but it cannot be expected to maintain the broad research commons from which the invention emerged. Venture capital rewards focus, speed, scalable demand, and an exit; these are often the opposite of portfolio breadth and patient capability-building.

Use Confidencial to describe the missing work between a paper or patent and a product: integration, usability, compliance, sales, procurement, customer support, and repeated redesign. This is not “non-research overhead”; it is a complementary transition system.

**Important balance:** Do not portray commercialization as contamination. The failure is asking commercialization vehicles to fund public goods for which they cannot capture a return.

### 11. Consolidation as Triage: SRI/PARC and IBM/HRL

**Function:** Return to the present after the incentive models are understood.

**Central claim:** Acquisition by a stronger research host may preserve people and facilities that otherwise face fragmentation. It can also narrow missions, centralize priority-setting, or preserve assets without preserving culture. The correct unit of analysis is what survives over time: teams, autonomy, technical infrastructure, customer relationships, and the ability to begin work whose payoff is distant.

Treat the two cases differently. PARC’s donation to SRI closed in 2023. As of July 23, 2026, IBM has announced and signed an agreement to acquire HRL, but the transaction remains subject to closing conditions and is expected to close later in 2026. Publish an update after closing rather than writing as though it has already occurred.

### 12. The Last Transfer Window

**Function:** Establish urgency without theatrical countdowns.

**Central claim:** The scarce resource is not merely money or young talent. It is the overlap between a cohort that carries tacit knowledge of durable laboratories and a cohort building with AI, new fabrication methods, quantum technologies, and new organizational tools.

Define the window as **one institutional generation**, not an unsupported calendar deadline. Investigate indicators:

- retirement and dispersal of senior technical staff;
- loss of apprenticeships and career technical tracks;
- closure or repurposing of specialized facilities;
- disappearance of internal archives, code, and negative results;
- concentration of frontier compute and instrumentation in a few firms;
- weakening of the organizations capable of absorbing large public investments.

**Global claim:** If the window closes, the cost will not be confined to U.S. competitiveness. The world loses institutions capable of producing foundational knowledge and trustworthy infrastructure whose benefits cannot be fully appropriated by their sponsors.

### 13. An AI-Native Laboratory for the Public Good

**Function:** End with an institutional design, not nostalgia or a slogan.

The successor should not be “Bell Labs, but with GPUs.” It should combine the best properties observed across the cases while explicitly correcting their incentive failures.

**Proposed form:** A federally chartered, independently governed nonprofit consortium with a long but reviewable mission charter; federal anchor funding and procurement; industry membership and transition partners; university training links; philanthropic room for unusually speculative work; and allied participation where openness and security permit.

**Government’s roles:**

- fund research whose spillovers exceed any one firm’s capturable return;
- provide long-lived compute, data, fabrication, test, and evaluation infrastructure;
- set mission challenges without specifying premature solutions;
- act as an early customer through procurement and advance commitments;
- maintain a portfolio across exploratory research, prototypes, and transition;
- preserve continuity between time-limited agency programs;
- require public-interest governance, evaluation, and eventual diffusion of appropriate results.

**Industry’s roles:**

- supply domain problems, engineers, data under controlled conditions, and transition paths;
- co-fund shared precompetitive infrastructure;
- rotate personnel through the laboratory without treating it as an outsourcing vendor;
- license and productize results under milestone-based terms.

**The AI-native operating system:**

1. **Institutional-memory layer:** Versioned hypotheses, decisions, experiments, failures, code, proofs, data provenance, and lessons—not merely final papers.
2. **Scientific-agent layer:** AI assistance for literature synthesis, conjecture generation, software, formal proof, simulation, experimental planning, and anomaly detection, with named human accountability.
3. **Secure resource layer:** Shared compute, privacy-preserving data access, reproducible environments, controlled research spaces, and auditable model use.
4. **Physical interface:** Automated laboratories, testbeds, semiconductor access, fabrication, robotics, and measurement so that AI is coupled to reality rather than confined to text.
5. **Human apprenticeship layer:** Long-lived technical teams in which junior researchers learn problem selection, experimental taste, systems judgment, and research ethics from experienced practitioners.
6. **Transition layer:** Embedded product engineers, standards experts, procurement specialists, and venture or licensing pathways from the beginning—not a technology-transfer office encountered at the end.

**AI’s proper role:** AI can widen the search space, lower the cost of synthesis and verification, preserve parts of institutional memory, and make small expert teams much more capable. It cannot supply the laboratory’s mandate, choose public priorities by itself, assume responsibility, build trust, or decide what risks society should take.

**IP compact:**

- foundational tools, standards, and non-sensitive results enter a research commons, possibly after a limited embargo;
- precompetitive work is shared among consortium members under transparent terms;
- company-specific development can remain proprietary when privately funded;
- exclusive licenses should be field-limited, time-limited, and tied to transition milestones, with reversion or march-in when results are warehoused;
- security-sensitive and dual-use work requires a separate controlled tier rather than using secrecy as the default for everything.

**Evaluation clocks:** Annual reviews should assess integrity and operational health; five-year reviews should assess capability formation and transition; ten- to fifteen-year reviews should assess mission-level effects. Publication and patent counts remain evidence, but not the objective function.

**Existing building blocks, not proof of completion:** The National AI Research Resource demonstrates shared public–private access to compute, data, tools, and training. The National Semiconductor Technology Center is designed as a public–private consortium for research, prototyping, commercialization, and workforce development. FFRDCs demonstrate long-term government relationships and institutional memory. DARPA demonstrates ambitious, empowered program leadership. DOE laboratories demonstrate facilities and technology-transfer mechanisms. The proposed laboratory should combine their complementary properties while studying their known governance and sustainability failures.

## Proof obligations for the book

The argument will be credible only if it answers strong objections.

1. **Aggregate-spending objection:** If U.S. R&D spending is at a record, exactly what was lost? Measure institutional form, concentration, research composition, continuity, and transition—not just totals.
2. **Survivorship-bias objection:** Were the classic labs genuinely better, or do we remember their successes and forget their waste and failures?
3. **Sector-shift objection:** Did research disappear, or move into software, biotechnology, hyperscalers, and newer corporate labs?
4. **Globalization objection:** Is this decline, or a broader and healthier international distribution of research capability?
5. **Technology objection:** Do cheaper compute, open source, AI, and distributed collaboration make large durable laboratories less necessary?
6. **Government-failure objection:** Why would public–private laboratories avoid bureaucracy, political capture, incumbent capture, duplication, and risk aversion?
7. **Counterexample objection:** What do IBM Research, Microsoft Research, Alphabet/DeepMind, national laboratories, successful FFRDCs, and emerging focused research organizations demonstrate that contradicts or narrows the thesis?

State in advance what evidence would weaken the thesis. A preordained elegy will be less persuasive than a proposition that survives attempted falsification.

## Evidence and interview method

### Four evidence classes

Tag working notes, even if the tags do not appear in the published prose:

- **M — Memory:** Direct personal observation, dated as closely as possible and bounded by role.
- **D — Document:** Annual reports, acquisition filings, budgets, contracts, public program pages, patents, publications, archived organization charts, and contemporaneous news.
- **I — Interview:** On-record, background, or confidential interview, with the status explicit in notes.
- **A — Analysis:** A causal inference or interpretation that follows from M, D, and I but is not itself directly observed.

Important claims should not rest on memory alone when documentary or interview corroboration is possible.

### Repeatable interview sample for each institution

Interview at least one person from each available perspective:

- senior researcher;
- early-career researcher or intern;
- technical manager;
- product, customer, or mission recipient;
- program manager or sponsor;
- transition, licensing, manufacturing, or business lead;
- someone who left during a contraction or reorganization.

Ask the same core questions: Who paid? What behavior was rewarded? What work could not be funded? What happened after a program ended? Which tool or team mattered but never appeared in an output metric? What was successfully transferred? What disappeared? What could not be rebuilt today?

### Personal archive to assemble

- a dated career and project chronology;
- public papers, patents, proposals, project announcements, and software releases;
- non-confidential photographs, badges, notebooks, calendars, and presentation decks;
- recollections of specific rooms, reviews, project starts, cancellations, and transition meetings;
- contemporaneous emails only when legally usable and free of protected information;
- a “scene bank” separating what was personally observed from what was learned later.

## Editorial form for every article

Target 2,500–3,500 words.

1. **Scene:** A concrete firsthand moment, 300–500 words.
2. **Claim:** The incentive mechanism the scene reveals.
3. **Institutional evidence:** Documents, data, and other witnesses.
4. **Technical example:** One project traced from question to outcome.
5. **Counterargument:** The strongest alternative explanation.
6. **Assessment:** Scientific, technical, transition, institutional, and public-value outcomes considered separately.
7. **Lesson:** What the case changes about the successor design.
8. **Open question:** A question inviting former colleagues and readers to contribute evidence.

The tone should be personal but not confessional, critical but not prosecutorial, and sobering without romanticizing monopoly or treating every acquisition as a funeral.

## Publication sequence

Publish every two or three weeks over seven to nine months.

- Publish Article 1 quickly enough to use the IBM–HRL announcement as the current hook.
- Publish Articles 2–3 as a paired Lucent/AT&T comparison.
- Publish Articles 4–8 as the institutional incentive cases, including the dedicated academic-system analysis.
- Pause after Article 8 to conduct follow-up interviews prompted by reader responses.
- Publish Articles 9–11 as the synthesis of metrics, transition, and consolidation.

Maintain a private book file in parallel. After each article, preserve cut material, contradictory evidence, interview excerpts, data tables, and unresolved questions. Those are book assets, not waste.

## Accuracy, confidentiality, and fairness rules

- Do not disclose proprietary, export-controlled, classified, customer-confidential, or NDA-covered information.
- Describe incentives and mechanisms rather than assigning motives to named individuals.
- Give institutions and interview subjects a chance to correct factual claims when warranted.
- Distinguish a personal view from an official institutional position.
- Do not imply firsthand knowledge of the 2023 SRI–PARC combination.
- Until the transaction closes, write “IBM announced an agreement to acquire HRL,” not “IBM acquired HRL” or “HRL is now IBM Research.”
- Reconcile the site’s inconsistent SRI end dates before publication.
- Use “twenty-five years of firsthand experience across a thirty-year institutional transformation,” unless earlier relevant experience supports a broader personal claim.

## The first paragraph to test

> In 2001, I arrived at Lucent Technologies carrying the usual assumptions of a young engineer: that important laboratories endure because their work is important, and that technical excellence is a form of institutional security. Lucent was already teaching a harsher lesson. A research culture can remain brilliant while the economic system that makes it possible is collapsing beneath it. Twenty-five years later, the names in my own career—Bell Labs, AT&T Labs, HRL, SRI, and now PARC and IBM—look less like a résumé than a record of how America repeatedly moved its capacity to think long from one institutional container to another. We did not stop doing R&D. We changed what the people paying for it were rewarded to preserve.

## The final proposition

The series should end on this claim:

> The United States cannot recreate the twentieth-century corporate laboratory by admiration, appropriation, or executive decree. But it may still be able to transfer the living knowledge of those institutions into an AI-native public–private successor. The window is narrow because institutional memory is perishable. The choice is not between restoring Bell Labs and accepting decline. It is between designing a new incentive system now and discovering later that money can purchase equipment, models, and projects—but not the lost ability to make them cohere across a generation.
